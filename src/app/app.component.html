If you can see where a line crosses the y-axis,
that is the y-intercept. In this image, the line
crosses the y-axis at (0,-2).
The y-intercept, then, is "-2".

Mathmatically, you can also think of it like this:
"if x=0, what is y".
The answer is your y-intercept.
See the Quick Maths tab -> Finding the y-intercept for more.

Think about a line as if it always "moves to the right."
if it is pointing in an "upward" direction, the slope is positive.
In this example we can easily see the "rise" and "run" of the line.
As it "rises" one unit, it will "run" 3 units.
The slope, then, is 1/3. Positive.
Think about a line as if it always "moves to the right."
if it is pointing in an "downward" direction, the slope is negative.
In this example we can easily see the "rise" and "run" of the line.
As it "rises" NEGATIVE one unit, it will "run" 2 units.
The slope, then, is -1/2. Negative.
There's a lot going on here... stay with me.

1. A "flat" line has a slope of 0. It never "rises", so it's
"rise/run" might look something like "0/5".

2. A "vertical" line has an undefined slope. It never "runs",
so it's "rise/run" might look something like "3/0" (divide by
zero error).

3. A line at a 45 degree angle has a slope of plus-or-minus 1.
Remember: "pointing down" is negative, "pointing up" is
positive.

4. A line that is closer to "flat" than 45 degrees, will have
a slope smaller than 1. REMEMBER: "pointing down" is negative,
"pointing up" is positive.

5. A line that is closer to "vertical" than 45 degrees will
have a slope bigger than 1. REMEMBER: "POINTING DOWN" IS
NEGATIVE, "POINTING UP" IS POSITIVE.
Remember: slope(m) = rise/run
rise = "change in y" -or- rise = y2-y1
run = "change in x" -or- run = x2-x1
m= (y2-y1) / (x2-x1)

In the example above, we chose (-8,6) as our first point and (-6,5) as our second point.
m= ((5)-(6)) / ((-6)-(-8))
m= (-1) / (2)
m = -1/2
If you know the slope(m), in this case it would be a "rise" of 1 over a "run" of -2 (-1/2):
You can plug any point into your slope-intercept formula (y = mx + b) to find b, which is your y-intercept.
Let's use (-6,5). When we solve for b we can insert y=5 and x=-6, because we know it's a point on our line.

So: 5=-1/2*-6 + b
Solve for b:
Simplifiy the right side [-1/2 * -6 = 3]
which gives us: 5 = 3 + b
Minus 3 from both sides to isolate b like: ((5)-3) = ((3+b) - 3)
Which gives us: 2 = b

Excellent! our y-intercept is 2.
